HAIID :: Prompt Theory I

Human-AI Interaction Design / IxD II / Feb 16–27, 2026

This module explores two fundamentally different relationships with AI:

Portfolio WITH an LLM — you design yourself to be seen by humans. The machine mirrors, interprets, surprises. The LLM is your collaborator.

Portfolio FOR an LLM — you design yourself to be seen by machines. You write for crawlers, bots, and summarizers. The LLM is your audience.

The needs are radically different. WITH requires trust, improvisation, and willingness to be surprised. FOR requires strategy, structure, and an understanding of how machines parse meaning. A good portfolio WITH an LLM might be messy, personal, full of unexpected connections. A good portfolio FOR an LLM must be clean, explicit, redundant — optimized for pattern-matching, not human aesthetics. In both cases, the prompt is not a command — it is a compositional act. The way you write to a machine matters as much as what you write.

Why This Module

As creative practitioners, your way of knowing is through making. Theory alone does not produce understanding — practice does.

Many of you hold thoughtful reservations about AI: its environmental cost, its labor implications, its entanglement with concentrated corporate power. Those reservations are not only valid — they are essential. This module does not ask you to set them aside. It asks you to bring them into the room.

But consider: can you meaningfully critique what you have not practiced? Can you design alternatives to systems you have not inhabited from the inside? The live coding community built a creative practice on corporate laptops running proprietary software — and transformed that practice into a site of collective experimentation and critical discourse. The question was never whether to engage with the machine, but how to engage with integrity.

We address ethics, ecology, labor, and infrastructure directly — in a dedicated morning segment every session. But we also practice. Because knowing-from-the-inside is different from knowing-about. Recent research in process-oriented prompting reframes the prompt not as a command but as a site of compositional practice — where rhythm, error, and improvisation become design material.

“The LLM is not a violin. It is a technology entangled with extractive labor practices, significant environmental cost, and concentrated corporate power. To propose reverence toward such a tool is not naïve; it is a deliberate provocation. Can reverence coexist with critique? Can you love the instrument and oppose the factory?”

— The Perfect Wrong Note

What we know from practice with these tools:

  • Error is material, not failure. Typos become grace notes. Degraded input opens unexpected semantic paths. You learn to integrate errors rather than avoid them — but only through practice.
  • Attunement requires repetition. The relationship a musician develops with an instrument through sustained practice. You cannot develop attunement with an LLM by reading about it.
  • The prompt is the creative act. In the age of generative AI, agency shifts from content to protocol. Meta-prompting — designing prompts that generate prompts — is design at the systems level.

Sessions

Session 1 — INSTALL

Mon Feb 16

Morning: Infrastructure

What runs under an LLM? Data centers, energy grids, fiber optics, cooling systems. The physical substrate of “intelligence.” We start here because you should know what you’re using before you use it.

Afternoon: Setup

Install VS Code, Cline extension. Configure ChatGPT API key (provided). Test your first prompt. Get comfortable in the terminal.

Before Monday, post two links in the Slack channel:

  • A link to something that represents what you’re most curious, excited, or interested about regarding AI
  • A link to something that represents what you’re most fearful, critical, or skeptical about regarding AI

Bring to class:

  • A laptop with VS Code installed (https://code.visualstudio.com/)
  • A tool you use (digital or physical)
  • An object you identify with

We record you talking about these things. The recordings become raw material for your portfolio.

Session 2 — VYPING

Wed Feb 18

Morning: Ethics

Consent, data ownership, representation. When you feed your voice to a transcription model, where does it go? Who benefits? We discuss the ethical dimensions of every tool we’ll use in this module.

Afternoon: Voice-to-text as creative method

Viping: typing at the speed of thought, without correction. The voice as input device. We show questions generated from your Day 1 recordings. Pair up. Interview each other. Vipe the answers (record → transcribe). Generate raw material for your portfolio.

Session 3 — Self-work

Thu Feb 19

Students work independently. Use your viped material. Start exploring how an LLM interprets you.

Session 4 — BRIEF: Portfolio WITH LLMs

Fri Feb 20

Morning: Ecology

The energy cost of a prompt. Water usage in data centers. Carbon footprint of training runs. We look at the numbers — and ask what “sustainable use” might mean for creative practitioners.

Afternoon: The brief

Introduce meta-prompting (prompts that generate prompts). Show examples. Technical support: how to feed viped data to an LLM, how to iterate, how to develop a creative relationship with the model.

Your deliverable: a portfolio created WITH an LLM. Format open — could be text, website, visual, audio. The LLM helps you see yourself. What does it notice? What does it emphasize? What surprises you?

Due Monday Feb 23.

Session 5 — PRESENTATIONS

Mon Feb 23

Morning: Labor

Who labels the training data? Who moderates the outputs? The invisible workforce behind AI systems. We read from and discuss the human labor embedded in every model.

Afternoon: Presentations

Each student presents their portfolio WITH an LLM. Discussion: What did the machine see that you didn’t? What did it miss? What did you learn about yourself through the process?

Session 6 — Portfolio FOR LLMs

Wed Feb 25

Morning: Infrastructure (protocol layer)

robots.txt, crawlers, web indexing. How machines read the internet. System prompting: hidden instructions that shape behavior. Your website as “system prompt for the internet.”

The key insight: you’re not writing for humans who will read your site. You’re writing for machines that will summarize your site to humans later.

Afternoon: The brief

Design a live website — your portfolio FOR an LLM. What does an LLM say about you when someone searches your name? What do you want it to say?

Principles for designing FOR machines:

  • Clarity over cleverness
  • Explicit over implied
  • Structured over stylish
  • Redundant over elegant (repetition helps pattern-matching)

Due Friday Feb 27.

Session 7 — Self-work

Thu Feb 26

Students work independently on final portfolio.

Session 8 — FINAL SHOWCASE

Fri Feb 27

Location: Dissection Room, University of Oslo

Morning: Open discussion

What did we learn? What changed? What are you still skeptical about?

Afternoon: Final presentations

Each student presents their live website — their portfolio FOR an LLM. Explain: Why is this a good “system prompt” for bots? What are you optimizing for? What did you leave out on purpose?

A space designed for looking closely at bodies. Clinical curiosity, not morbid fascination. You’ve spent two weeks examining how machines see you — and designing how they should.
